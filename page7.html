Lab 3 - “I came, I saw, I soldered”

Description

Lab 3 introduces the basics of soldering. Soldering is a technique to connect two pieces of metal together by melting another metal/alloy (Solder) between them. Solder has a much lower melting point than other electrical components that get connected. Soldering is extremely useful in electrical prototyping.

Materials Used

  • Arduino (Elegoo) Uno R3 Controller Board
  • USB Cable
  • USB Adapter (if you have a USB-C-only laptop)
  • Button
  • Breadboard Jumper Wire
  • Resistors
  • LEDs
  • Multimeter
  • Protoboard (Not in the kit; provided)
  • Stranded and Solid-core Wires (Not in the kit; provided)
  • Soldering Iron (BTU)
  • Solder Wire (Lead-free, rosin core) (BTU)
  • Fume Extractor (BTU)
  • Brass Wire Pad (BTU)
  • Soldering Helping Hands (BTU)
  • Solder Sucker Desoldering Pump (BTU)
  • Solder Wick (BTU)
  • Wire Stripper (BTU)
  • Diagonal Pliers (BTU)
  • Safety Glasses (BTU)

Part 1: Soldering Setup and Safety

I first assembled all of the tools I needed to start soldering. This included the soldering iron, fume extractor, brass wire pad, helping hands, solder sucker, wick, wire stripper, diagonal plier, solder wire, and safety glasses. A picture is shown below.

Soldering Safety Steps

  1. Assemble all of the necessary tools as pictured above
  2. Turn the soldering iron ON using switch and ensure the temperature is between 600 - 700 degrees farenheit
  3. Be very careful while handling the soldering iron because the tip is extremely hot
  4. Always grab the soldering iron using the handle and not the front metal part
  5. Never keep the soldering iron outside its stand - don't put it directly on the table or some other surface
  6. Once the soldering iron is turned on, turn on the fume extractor fan
  7. Always remember to wash your hands with soap and water after soldering
  8. DO NOT EAT OR DRINK NEAR THE SOLDERING STATION
  9. Always wear safety glasses to protect your eyes

Part 2: Soldering Wires to Protoboard

For this part of the lab, I began by tinning the soldering iron. I repeated the tinning and cleaning treatment every few minutes while working on this lab. Below is a picture of the tinned and cleaned-up soldering iron tip.

Next, I took a 4-inch piece of stranded wire and stripped off about 2 mm of one of the ends using the wire stripper. I rolled the strands together to make them adhere to each other. Next, I inserted the metallic part through one of the corner holes of the protoboard. Finally, I soldered the joint together by applying solder to the tip of the soldering iron and bringing the tip to the top of the exposed metallic part. I repeated this process for the other end of the stranded wire and for two more solid-core wires. I ended with the protoboard pictured below.

To me, the type of wire that was easier to solder was the solid-core. The stranded wire kept separating despite my best efforts, so it was more difficult to get through the holes in the protoboard.

Part 3: Bridge of Solder

In this part of the lab, I conneted the joints made in the previous part to each other by trace soldering. I applied solder on two adjacent holes in the protoboard, then I deposited more solder onto them while molding the solder to form a bridge between the two holes. I repeated this until I had three traces, pictured below.

Part 4: Splicing Wires

For this part of the lab, I connected pieces of wire by splicing them. I combined four 2-inch wires to create one long 8-inch piece of wire with three splice joints. In order to splice the wires, I followed the following steps:

  1. Strip the ends
  2. Hold the individual pieces upright using helping hands, and apply solder to the exposed metal in the wires.
  3. Position the two wires such that the two exposed ends with solder are barely touching each other.
  4. Hold them steady while touching the point of contact with the soldering iron such that the solder melts on both wires and combines to form a bigger blob of solder.
  5. Once you see that the solder blob mostly covers the junction of the two wires, move the solder away and hold the wires steady until the solder completely solidifies.

Part 4: LED Reaction Game

This is my code for the LED Reaction Game. To explain the program flow, the game overall starts with a welcome and ready statement that only prints once becasue it is in the setup() function. Once the program moves on the loop function, it uses digitalRead to check if the input pin is HIGH or LOW. While it is LOW, it calculates a random number between .5 seconds and 5 seconds, then inputs that number into the delay. Once the delay is done, the program caluclates the time it has been since the program started, and it turns on the LED using digitalWrite. Once the user pushes the button, the program moves to the second while loop. Here, it turns the LED off using digitalWrite. Then, it calculates the time it took to press the button using the "startTime" measurement from the first while loop. Finally, it prints the reaction time and implements a 5 second delay for the player to get ready.

Part 2: (Not) Blowing Up An LED

This portion of the lab required calculating the amount of resistance required to properly set up a circuit with a LED. A resistor is required to control the current flowing through the LED, so that it doesn't 'blow up' from too much current. The calculation for the amount of resistance required can be performed using Ohm's Law: V = IR. The LEDs I used for this lab had a max current rating of 40 mA, so a current of about 20 mA t0 35 mA was appropriate for safely running the LED. Below is the calculation provided for determining the resistance requried when powering the LED with a 3.3V source.

V = I * R

R = V / I

R = 3.3V / 30 mA = 110 Ohm

Because I did not have a 110 Ohm reistor, I set up the circuit using a 100 Ohm resistor. Below is a schematic of the circuit and a picture of the physical circuit I created.

I next created a circuit for the LED using the 5V power source. I started by calculating the resistance required using a 5V power sources and a current of 20 mA. Then, I calculate the resistance required using a 5V power sources and a current of 35 mA. I averaged the two resistance values, and I chose a resistor that was close to that average. Below are my calculations.

V = I * R

R = V / I

R = 5V / 20 mA = 250 Ohm

R = 5V / 35 mA = 140 Ohm

Average = ((250 Ohm + 140 Ohm) / 2) = 195 Ohm

Because I did not have a 195 Ohm reistor, I set up the circuit using a 220 Ohm resistor. Below is a schematic of the circuit and a picture of the physical circuit I created.
